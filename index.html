01 The Experience

You are Sarah.

"That's not distance chosen deliberately. That's depletion."

The user embodies Sarah, a firefighter's partner, watching a Tuesday evening unfold after James comes home from a difficult shift. Through voice, objects, and the silence between them, the experience communicates what Operational Stress Injuries look like inside a home and connects families to Wounded Warriors Canada's support programs.

We chose not to show the job. We show what it leaves behind.

02 How We Got Here

A complete rebuild.

V1 had eight scenes across multiple locations. Users could not follow it. They focused on completing tasks rather than feeling anything. We did not patch it. We rebuilt it from the ground up.

V1 Problem

Eight scenes. Multiple locations. No narrative thread.

Users were completing tasks to advance rather than engaging with the story. The fire station scene was not recognizable. The flashback felt like showing trauma rather than understanding it. The cup metaphor added complexity without clarity.

Before
V2 Solution

One home. One evening. Every object carries meaning.

We collapsed everything into a single home that evolves through frame transitions. A full narrator speaks to the user as Sarah throughout. Every interaction is tied to the narrative: putting a coat away, clearing a cold plate, reading an unopened message.

After
Why No Characters

Uncanny valley breaks immersion before it begins.

Through our course and instructor feedback we learned that character models in VR risk creating an uncanny valley effect where almost human is not quite human enough, which pulls users out of the experience. We let voice, objects, and spatial audio do the work instead.

Design Decision
What Testing Changed

Real input from a 911 Operator and active firefighters.

Our 911 Operator said the flashback would be giving them trauma too. Firefighters on Reddit confirmed the toll is accumulation over years, not one dramatic call. Every major design change traces back to a real person's real words.

Scene 06 The Choice Explained

How would you respond?

In the final scene, three response bubbles appear. The user selects what they believe is the most supportive first step. All three responses come from a place of care. None of them are wrong. But none of them will immediately fix what Operational Stress Injury builds up over years. What they can do is signal safety, reduce pressure, and create the conditions for healing over time.

Panel A
Best First Step

"I'm here, you don't have to carry this alone."

Presence without pressure or expectation. COPE identifies this as the strongest opening move. It communicates that you are not going anywhere, without asking anything in return. It does not promise a fix. It offers a foundation.

Panel B

"Whenever you're ready to talk, I'm here."

Caring and patient, but naming the act of talking can introduce subtle pressure. It frames the goal as a conversation rather than just being together. Good language for a later moment, once the person already feels safe enough to consider talking.

Panel C

"What if we looked into some support together? I'll come with you."

A meaningful and concrete offer, and ultimately an important one. But it arrives before presence has been established. Moving to solutions too early can feel like the other person wants to resolve the discomfort rather than sit with it. This response belongs a step or two later.

None of these responses will undo an Operational Stress Injury overnight. OSI develops over years of accumulated exposure, and recovery follows a similar pace. What a partner can offer is consistency, patience, and the willingness to stay present without needing to fix anything immediately. That is what the Spousal Resiliency Program and COPE are built around.

Every object in the experience is a stand-in for something that cannot be said out loud.

06 WWC Connection

Built around real programs.

Every scene connects to a specific Wounded Warriors Canada program. This is not just an awareness campaign. It tells families where to go next.

🤝

Spousal Resiliency Program

Named through the dinner plate interaction. The narrator says directly: you are carrying something too, and you deserve support just as much as he does.

💬

COPE

Couples Overcoming PTSD Every Day. Connected to the choice panels. Presence over fixing. Staying without requiring explanation. The correct response reflects what COPE teaches.

🏠

Surviving Family Program

Named in the outro alongside the other programs. All three are displayed before woundedwarriors.ca appears on screen so every user leaves with somewhere to go.

07 Design Process

How we designed it.

Every decision came from someone telling us something real. The process was iterative, grounded in research, and when we needed to find voices outside our usual channels, resourceful.

01
Client Brief

Wounded Warriors Canada

Wounded Warriors Canada set three clear priorities from the start: no graphic incident depiction, no risk of retraumatizing families, and WWC's programs integrated meaningfully throughout. Those three principles became the filter for every design decision that followed.

02
First Responder Research

911 Operator Interview and Reddit Outreach

After Waterloo Region Fire Department and Waterloo Regional Police Service did not respond to our outreach, we posted on r/Firefighting. The post reached over 3,800 views. A 911 Operator gave us a full structured interview. Two active firefighters from Reddit shaped how we framed James's behaviour throughout the evening scenes.

03
User Testing

Five Participants, Think Aloud Method

The morning scene, the phone interaction, the choice panel language, and the removal of the flashback all came directly from participant feedback. The 911 Operator said the flashback would be giving them trauma too. That was enough.

04
Spatial Audio Strategy

Voice and Sound Over Characters

We chose not to use character models to avoid uncanny valley effects, a principle from our course and reinforced by instructor feedback. Sarah's footsteps from the hallway, James's voice from the armchair, the door closing from the left. Spatial audio creates presence where visible characters would have broken it.
